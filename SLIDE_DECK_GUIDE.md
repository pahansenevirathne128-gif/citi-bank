# Citi Global Markets Challenge 2026 — Slide-by-Slide Guide

> **Deck structure:** 17 slides across four acts — Context, The Four Cascades, Portfolio Construction, and Risk Management. Each slide has one job. Read the "What it teaches" column before writing the narrative.

---

## Act I — Context & Thesis (Slides 1–5)

---

### Slide 1 — Title & Executive Summary

**Headline:** *"The Second-Order Trade: Four Cascades the Market Has Missed"*

**What it teaches:**
The opening frame. It tells the audience in one sentence what the entire pitch is about — not the Iran war itself (everyone knows that), but the *downstream effects* that haven't priced yet. The summary should state the portfolio size ($500M long/short), the benchmark (Fund X), and the four cascade themes by name. Think of it as the abstract of a research paper: a judge should be able to read only this slide and understand the entire argument.

**Key message to land:** We are not trading the event. We are trading the market's failure to follow through on its own implications.

---

### Slide 2 — Hero 1: The Divergence Chart

**Chart:** `charts/hero_1_divergence.png`
**Title:** *"Fertilizer is up 49%. Corn is up 0.5%."*

**What it teaches:**
This is the single most important visual in the deck. It earns credibility immediately by showing a concrete, measurable anomaly — not a macro opinion. Urea and ammonia have moved sharply; grain prices have not. The indexed lines (base = Jan 2025) make the gap undeniable. A judge looking at this chart for 10 seconds should think: *something has to give.*

**Key message to land:** The divergence is real, quantified, and historically unprecedented in duration. This is not a thesis — it is a fact chart.

---

### Slide 3 — The Macro Setup: Why 2026 is Different

**What it teaches:**
Bridges from the opening hook (Slide 2) to the full argument. Explains why the 2026 Iran conflict is a structurally different shock from prior Middle East crises: it combined a commodity supply shock *and* a European security shock *and* a monetary policy shock simultaneously. Most macro shocks affect one dimension. This one hit three at once — which is why the second-order effects are so much larger and so much slower to price.

**Key message to land:** Three simultaneous shocks → three separate repricing lags → four distinct alpha opportunities.

---

### Slide 4 — First-Order Effects: What's Already Priced

**What it teaches:**
A critical credibility slide. It explicitly concedes what *has* priced correctly — long oil, long gold, short equities on the Iran shock. This disarms the obvious objection ("isn't this all already in the market?") before a judge can raise it. Shows the audience the team understands what the market got *right* before explaining what it got *wrong*.

**Key message to land:** We are not arguing with the market's first reaction. We are arguing that the market stopped thinking after step one.

---

### Slide 5 — The Opportunity Map: Four Cascades, One Portfolio

**What it teaches:**
The architecture slide. A single 2×2 or 4-row table that names each cascade, identifies the asset pair with the pricing gap, and states the expected convergence timeline. This is the roadmap for slides 7–10. Judges should be able to flip back to this slide at any point and understand where they are in the argument.

| Cascade | Gap | Timeline |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Fertilizer → Grain | +49% vs +0.5% | 6–12 weeks |
| 2. Defence backlogs → ETF re-rating | +36% backlog vs −4% ETF | 1–2 quarters |
| 3. Commodity FX → Importer FX | Divergence at 18-month high | 4–8 weeks |
| 4. Sticky CPI → Fed repricing | 3.9% CPI vs 0.5 cuts priced | 1 FOMC cycle |

**Key message to land:** Each cascade is independent. If only two of four converge, the portfolio still wins.

## Act II — The Four Cascades (Slides 6–10)

---

### Slide 6 — Cascade Framework: How Lags Create Alpha

**What it teaches:**
Before diving into each cascade, this slide explains *why* these gaps exist at all — why doesn't the market price second-order effects instantly? Three structural reasons: (1) commodity and equity desks don't talk to each other; (2) supply chain lags are real, not just perceptual; (3) institutional investors anchor to the first narrative (long oil) and are slow to rotate. This slide transforms what might look like "the market is stupid" into a rigorous argument about *mechanism*.

**Key message to land:** These are not mispricings caused by ignorance. They are caused by structural fragmentation in how markets process multi-leg shocks.

---

### Slide 7 — Cascade 1: The Fertilizer–Grain Lag (Historical Evidence)

**Chart:** `charts/cascade_1_fertilizer_lag.png`
**Title:** *"Three crises. Same lag. Same trade."*

**What it teaches:**
Evidence-based validation of Cascade 1. Instead of asserting that grain must catch up to fertilizer, it *proves* that this has happened in every comparable historical episode — 1973, 2008, and 2022 — with a consistent 6–12 week lag. The grey historical lines plus the 2026 live overlay is the rhetorical structure: the audience sees pattern recognition, not prediction. The vertical line at week 8 ("we are here") creates urgency — this is a live trade, not a retrospective.

**Key message to land:** History doesn't guarantee the outcome but it narrows the confidence interval dramatically. We are at exactly the point where grain has moved in all three prior episodes.

---

### Slide 8 — Cascade 2: The Defence Backlog Anomaly

**Chart:** `charts/cascade_2_defence_backlog.png`
**Title:** *"Backlogs +36%. Stocks −4% YTD. Something has to give."*

**What it teaches:**
A classic fundamental vs. price divergence. Order backlogs are a leading indicator of future revenue — they are contractually committed, not forecast. When Rheinmetall's backlog grows 36% YoY and the EUAD ETF is down 4%, one of them is wrong. This chart makes the case visually with the dual-axis bar + line structure. The Rheinmetall bar highlighted in gold draws the eye to the strongest data point.

**Key message to land:** Backlog growth is not sentiment — it is signed contracts. The equity market has not yet connected the earnings dots.

---

### Slide 9 — Cascade 3: The FX Dislocation (PCA Biplot)

**Chart:** `charts/hero_2_fx_pca.png`
**Title:** *"Commodity FX is diverging. Asian importers are not keeping up."*

**What it teaches:**
The most technically sophisticated slide in the deck — and intentionally so, as this is the "quant" section. The PCA biplot compresses 9 FX pairs into a two-dimensional story: commodity exporters (AUD, CAD, NOK, ZAR) are clustering in one direction; Asian importers (KRW, IDR, THB, INR, PHP) are clustering in the opposite. The gap between the two clusters represents a structural FX divergence driven by the commodity shock. The variance-explained annotation (top two components) validates that the visual isn't noise.

**Key message to land:** This divergence is not visible in any single FX pair — it requires a multi-dimensional view to see. That is why it hasn't been arb'd yet.

---

### Slide 10 — Cascade 4: Sticky Inflation vs. Fed Pricing

**Chart:** `charts/cascade_4_sticky_cpi.png`
**Title:** *"CPI is sticky. Markets price 0.5 cuts. Gap = trade."*

**What it teaches:**
The macro rates leg of the portfolio. Atlanta Fed Sticky CPI measures prices that change slowly — services, rents, wages — and it is running at ~3.9%. The market is pricing only 12.5bps of Fed cuts in 2026. The dual-axis chart shows both series converging toward a clear implication: either the Fed cuts and re-ignites inflation, or they stay hawkish and the market's cut pricing is wrong. Either way, TIPS outperforms nominal bonds.

**Key message to land:** Sticky CPI is the Fed's binding constraint. The market hasn't accepted this yet. Our TIPS overweight is the structural hedge.

## Act III — Portfolio Construction (Slides 11–14)

---

### Slide 11 — Black-Litterman Portfolio: Posterior Weights

**Chart:** `charts/portfolio_risk_donut.png`
**Data:** `data/portfolio_weights.csv`
**Title:** *"Risk budget: commodity and defence dominate."*

**What it teaches:**
Transforms the four qualitative cascade views into a rigorous quantitative portfolio using the Black-Litterman framework. The donut chart shows *marginal risk contribution* — not just weights — which is the correct way to show how a portfolio allocates risk rather than capital. The table comparing equal-weight vs Fund X vs BL posterior makes the tilt explicit. Commodities and TIPS absorb the most risk budget, consistent with the cascade thesis.

**Key message to land:** This is not a discretionary tilt. The Black-Litterman framework forces each view to compete on confidence and impact. The portfolio is only as aggressive as the evidence warrants.

| Asset | Fund X | BL Posterior | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|---|
| US Equities | 30% | 0% | Maximum underweight — crowded long |
| EU Equities | 20% | 8% | Underweight despite defence tailwind |
| EM Equities | 0% | 0% | Avoid: importer FX headwind |
| IG Bonds | 30% | 34% | Slight overweight, duration hedge |
| TIPS | 0% | 21% | Cascade 4 expression |
| Commodities | 20% | 37% | Core cascade 1 + 3 expression |

---

### Slide 12 — Benchmark Deep Dive: Understanding Fund X

**What it teaches:**
Explains the benchmark to judges who may not be familiar with the blended construction (50% MSCI World / 30% FTSE WBIG / 20% S&P GSCI). This is important because the portfolio's *relative* return story only makes sense if the audience understands what Fund X holds. Key point: Fund X has zero FX alpha and zero inflation protection — our portfolio attacks both of those gaps directly.

**Key message to land:** Fund X is a buy-and-hold passive allocation. It has no mechanism to benefit from any of the four cascades. Our edge is structural, not directional.

---

### Slide 13 — Hero 3: Backtest Stress Matrix

**Charts:** `charts/hero_3_backtest.png`, `charts/hero_3_stress_table.png`
**Data:** `data/backtest_stress_matrix.csv`
**Title:** *"In every commodity-geopolitical analogue, our portfolio outperforms."*

**What it teaches:**
The most important risk credibility slide. Uses four historical analogue periods (1973, 2008, 2011, 2022) to show how a portfolio structured like ours would have performed vs. Fund X in comparable regimes. The 4-panel drawdown chart makes the outperformance visual; the table quantifies it. The caveat ("asset-class proxies, ±300bps") is intentionally visible — it builds trust with quantitatively literate judges.

| Period | Fund X | Our Portfolio | Delta |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1973 Oil Embargo | −7.8% | −2.2% | +560bps |
| 2008 Commodity Spike | +1.5% | +9.5% | +800bps |
| 2011 Arab Spring | +2.6% | +6.5% | +390bps |
| 2022 Russia-Ukraine | −9.5% | −2.3% | +714bps |

**Key message to land:** The portfolio does not just outperform in bull markets. It outperforms in stress scenarios — which is exactly what the competition is testing for.

---

### Slide 14 — Portfolio Summary: Returns, Risk, and Sizing

**What it teaches:**
The consolidated portfolio scorecard. Expected return, volatility, Sharpe ratio, maximum drawdown, and gross/net exposure for the full $500M portfolio. Also shows the three tail hedges: (1) long vol on oil (protects if cascade 1 reverses), (2) long USD puts against KRW (protects if FX dislocation closes the wrong way), (3) long CDS on European sovereign (protects if defence budgets trigger fiscal stress). Each hedge is sized to cap the worst-case loss at a defined threshold.

**Key message to land:** We know exactly what can go wrong. The hedges are not afterthoughts — they are integral to the portfolio construction.

## Act IV — Risk, Monitoring & Submission (Slides 15–17)

---

### Slide 15 — Execution Plan: How We Actually Trade This

**What it teaches:**
Operationalises the theory. Specifies the exact instruments for each cascade leg — futures, ETFs, FX forwards, options — and the entry/exit conditions. This slide answers the judge's implicit question: "This sounds good in theory, but how do you actually put it on?" Sizing is shown as % of NAV with rationale for each position limit.

**Key message to land:** Every position has a defined entry, a defined exit trigger, and a maximum size. This is a trading plan, not a research note.

---

### Slide 16 — Risk Panel: Live Ceasefire Probability & Scenario Analysis

**What it teaches:**
The key risk to the entire thesis is an Iran ceasefire — it would partially unwind the commodity shock and reduce the fertilizer divergence. This slide monitors that risk in real time using Polymarket ceasefire probability data. It also shows a three-scenario stress table: (A) no ceasefire — full cascade convergence; (B) partial ceasefire — two of four cascades unwind; (C) full ceasefire — portfolio drawdown estimate and hedge payoff. Scenario analysis proves the team has stress-tested the downside.

**Key message to land:** The biggest risk to the trade is the trade resolving. We have sized and hedged accordingly. A ceasefire is a known risk with a known impact — it is not a tail risk, it is the central scenario to monitor.

---

### Slide 17 — Appendix: Methodology & Data Sources

**What it teaches:**
The technical credibility anchor. Judges who want to drill into the quant work can find it here. Contains five sub-sections:

- **A. Fertilizer-Grain Model** — VECM specification, data sources (CNBC, IFPRI, yfinance), 2022 analogue justification
- **B. FX PCA Methodology** — 2-year return window, StandardScaler, sign convention, full loadings table
- **C. Backtest Caveats** — asset-class proxy construction, cascade alpha calibration, ±300bps uncertainty range
- **D. Black-Litterman Specification** — P matrix, Q vector, confidence levels, tau parameter, Ledoit-Wolf covariance
- **E. References** — Bloomberg, IFPRI, Atlanta Fed, CME FedWatch, Polymarket, yfinance tickers

**Key message to land:** Every number in this deck has a source. Every model has stated assumptions. Every caveat is explicit.

## Narrative Thread (the red line through the deck)

Every slide should answer one of four questions:

1. **"What is the opportunity?"** → Slides 1–5
2. **"Why does the gap exist and how do we know it will close?"** → Slides 6–10
3. **"How do we size and express the trade?"** → Slides 11–14
4. **"What kills the trade and how do we manage it?"** → Slides 15–17

If a slide cannot be assigned to one of these four questions, it probably doesn't belong in the deck.
